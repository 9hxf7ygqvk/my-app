Personal Curriculum — Draft 1

A Blueprint for
Self-Education

Three parallel tracks. No deadlines, no grades. Just a structured path from where you are to where you want to be — across math, language, and the hard work of knowing yourself.

01 / Mathematics 02 / Language Arts 03 / Self-Discovery
How this works

Three tracks running in parallel. Every day you touch all three — but the time is short enough to not feel overwhelming. Think of it like a workout split: each muscle group gets trained, nothing gets neglected.

Track 01
Mathematics
Arithmetic → Pre-Algebra → Algebra 1 → Geometry. Fill every gap on the way up. Built for creative use later.
Track 02
Language Arts
Reading closely, taking real notes, writing expressively. From essay fundamentals to finding your own voice.
Track 03
Self-Discovery
Structured reflection, philosophy primers, opinion auditing. Figure out what you actually think — and why.
Daily structure (≈ 2 hrs)
30–45 min Math — Khan Academy, in order. One concept at a time.
30–40 min Reading + Writing — One short read, then write your reaction.
20–30 min Journal / Reflection — After something hits you, write it down.
Remaining Scrimba / Creative — Whatever energy is left. No pressure.
The goal isn't speed. The goal is that six months from now you can read something complex and understand it, write something and mean it, do math without flinching, and know yourself well enough to make real decisions. That's it.
01
Mathematics
You're not behind — you just have gaps. This track fills them in order, so nothing breaks later. The destination is geometry, but the real prize is understanding numbers well enough to use them creatively.
◎ Main tool: Khan Academy (free, adaptive, tracks your progress)
Phase 1
Arithmetic Foundations
Estimated: 2–4 weeks
Division — long division, remainders, dividing decimalsStart here. Do the Khan exercises until they feel automatic, not just familiar.
Fractions — adding, subtracting, multiplying, dividing fractionsThis is the most important phase. Almost every math problem you'll ever do touches fractions.
Decimals & Percentages — converting between all three formsFractions, decimals, and percentages are the same thing in three outfits. Understand the relationship.
Negative numbers — the number line, operations with negativesSounds basic. Gets everyone. Do it properly.
Order of operations — PEMDAS, what it means and why it exists
Where to go
Khan Academy → "Arithmetic" unit (search it directly)
Do the practice exercises, not just the videos. The exercises are the actual learning.
Phase 2
Pre-Algebra
Estimated: 3–5 weeks
Variables and expressions — what a variable actually is, simplifyingThis is the shift from arithmetic to algebra. A lot of people skip it and then algebra makes no sense.
Equations — one-step, two-step, solving for xThink of an equation as a balance scale. Whatever you do to one side, you do to the other.
Ratios and proportions — rates, unit rates, proportional relationships
Basic geometry concepts — angles, lines, area, perimeter, volumePreview of what's coming. Don't go deep yet — just get familiar with the shapes and vocab.
Exponents & roots — squares, cubes, square rootsYou'll need these constantly in algebra and geometry.
Where to go
Khan Academy → "Pre-algebra" course
YouTube: Professor Leonard's pre-algebra playlist (longer, but deeper)
Phase 3
Algebra 1
Estimated: 6–10 weeks
Linear equations and inequalities — systems of equations, graphing linesGraphing a line is just visualizing a relationship. This is where math starts becoming visual.
Functions — what a function is, function notation, input/outputFunctions are everywhere in programming and generative art. This one matters for your other goals too.
Polynomials — adding, subtracting, multiplyingThe building blocks of most algebra that follows.
Factoring — GCF, factoring trinomials, difference of squares
Quadratic equations — FOIL, the quadratic formula, parabolasThis is where algebra gets genuinely interesting. Parabolas show up in physics, art, and architecture.
Where to go
Khan Academy → "Algebra 1" course
Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) — for going deeper on any specific topic
Phase 4
Geometry
Estimated: 6–8 weeks
Points, lines, planes, angles — the foundational vocabularyGeometry has its own language. Learn it first or everything else is confusing.
Triangles — types, congruence, similarity, the Pythagorean theoremThe Pythagorean theorem shows up in 3D graphics, game dev, physics, and generative art constantly.
Circles — circumference, area, arc length, radiansRadians are how computers think about rotation. This directly connects to creative coding.
Coordinate geometry — distance formula, midpoint, slope on a plane
Transformations — translation, rotation, reflection, scaleThis is the math behind every animation and generative artwork. This is where it clicks.
Proofs (light) — basic two-column proofs, logical reasoningDon't stress proofs. But doing a few teaches you to think rigorously.
Where to go
Khan Academy → "Geometry" course
3Blue1Brown on YouTube — for visual intuition on anything that confuses you
How to actually study math
Do the exercises first, then watch the video if you're stuck — not the other way around.
Write things out by hand. There's something about handwriting math that makes it stick differently.
If you get something wrong, don't skip it. Figure out exactly why you got it wrong.
30 minutes a day consistently beats 3 hours on a Saturday every time.
Once you hit geometry, try sketching the problems visually before solving them.
02
Language Arts
Reading to actually understand. Writing to actually say something. The goal isn't to sound smart — it's to be able to put exactly what you mean into words, the way a painting puts a feeling into color.
◎ End goal: Read anything, understand it deeply, write about it in your own voice
Phase 1
Reading with Intention
Start now, runs through everything
Active reading habit — read something every day, even if it's shortStart with essays and long articles, not books. They're self-contained and easier to finish.
Annotation — underline, circle unfamiliar words, write in marginsIf you're reading on a screen, use the highlights tool or paste key lines into a notes doc.
Main idea vs detail — identify what the piece is actually arguing, vs what's supporting itMost people read the details and miss the argument. Ask: what is this person trying to say?
Vocabulary in context — when you hit an unknown word, try to infer the meaning first, then look it upDon't skip words. Every word you understand is a word you can eventually use.
Good places to read
The Atlantic, Aeon, The Paris Review (free essays online)
Wikipedia "deep dives" — pick a topic you're curious about, follow links
Project Gutenberg — free classic books, especially short novellas
After reading, write 3–5 sentences
What did this piece argue or show?
What's one thing you agreed with, one thing you pushed back on?
What question does it leave you with?
Phase 2
Note-Taking Systems
Weeks 2–4
The Cornell Method — divide your page: notes on the right, key questions on the left, summary at the bottomGood for structured content like textbooks, articles with clear points.
The "In Your Own Words" rule — never copy text into notes. Paraphrase everything.If you can't paraphrase it, you haven't understood it yet.
Linking ideas — after notes from different sources, try connecting them: how does this reading relate to that one?This is where actual thinking starts happening.
Review practice — go back over notes 24 hrs later and write one sentence summarizing what you'd keep
Phase 3
Writing Fundamentals
Weeks 3–8, ongoing
The sentence — what makes a sentence clear, what makes it interestingMost bad writing is bad at the sentence level. Read your sentences out loud. If it's awkward spoken, it's awkward written.
The paragraph — one idea per paragraph, topic sentence, supporting detail, closing thoughtA paragraph is a unit of thought. If you have two ideas, use two paragraphs.
The basic essay structure — intro (what you're arguing + why), body (your reasoning), conclusion (what it means)Essays are just organized thinking. The structure exists to help the reader follow you, not to constrain you.
Voice — writing in a way that sounds like you, not like a textbookDon't write how you think you're supposed to write. Write how you'd explain it to someone you respect.
Resources
On Writing Well — William Zinsser (the clearest book on clear writing)
Strunk & Elements of Style — short, free online, skip the style rules, read the clear thinking advice
Khan Academy → "Writing" unit for essay structure basics
Phase 4
Expressive Writing
Month 2 onward
Personal essay — write about something that happened to you and what it meantThe personal essay is where writing and self-discovery overlap. It's also the hardest form to fake.
Lyric essay — non-linear, image-driven, more like a painting than an argumentThis might be the form closest to what you described wanting. Look up Maggie Nelson's "Bluets" as an example.
Critical writing — write about a piece of art, music, or book and argue why it matters (or doesn't)
Stream of consciousness practice — 10 minutes, write without stopping, don't editNot meant to be good. Meant to get the internal monologue onto paper so you can see what it actually looks like.
Weekly writing practice
Mon/Wed/Fri: Short writes (1 paragraph, react to something you read or saw)
Once a week: Longer piece (1–2 pages, any form you want)
Don't delete anything. Even bad drafts are data.
03
Self-Discovery
Most people go their whole lives never seriously asking: do I believe this because I thought it through, or because I absorbed it? This track is about developing the habit of asking that question — and being honest enough to sit with the answer.
◎ End goal: Know what you actually think, where it came from, and why it might be wrong
Phase 1
The Reflection Journal
Start now, runs forever
Keep a private doc or physical notebook — not for anyone else, not to postIt only works if it's completely private. The moment you're writing for an audience, you start performing.
The two-question habit — after anything that triggers a reaction (video, conversation, art): "What do I actually think?" then "Why do I think that?"The second question is the one that does the work.
Opinion audit — once a week, pick one opinion you hold strongly and argue the other side as convincingly as you canIf you can't steelman the opposing view, you don't understand your own position yet.
Trace your influences — when you notice a belief or taste, ask: where did I first get this?Most of what we think we chose, we absorbed. That's fine — but it's worth knowing.
Journal prompts to start with
"Something I believed last year that I'm not sure about anymore..."
"A thing I find beautiful that I can't fully explain why..."
"An opinion I hold that I think I got from [person/place] — and whether I actually agree..."
"Something I avoid thinking about and why..."
"What kind of person do I want to be in 5 years? What would that person think about how I'm spending today?"
Phase 2
Philosophy Primers
Month 1–2, ≈20 min/week
Epistemology basics — how do we know what we know? What makes a belief justified?This is directly relevant to figuring out whether your opinions are actually yours.
Stoic basics — Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, what you control vs what you don'tNot for motivation. For a framework for thinking about your situation clearly and without drama.
Ethics 101 — consequentialism, deontology, virtue ethics (not to memorize, to have a vocabulary for moral thinking)Gives you language for arguments you're already having internally.
Existentialism light — Sartre's "existence precedes essence", Camus on absurdityDon't get lost here. Read, journal about it, move on. The point is exposure, not mastery.
Where to start
Crash Course Philosophy (YouTube) — dense but fast, good overview
Marcus Aurelius: Meditations — read a few pages at a time, not cover to cover
The School of Life (YouTube) — accessible, visual, on specific thinkers
Phase 3
Taste & Identity Mapping
Ongoing from month 2
Build a "things I love" archive — images, music, writing, art, ideas that you react to viscerallyThe goal is to look for patterns. What do the things you love have in common? That tells you something real.
Write about your taste — pick something you love and try to articulate exactly what about it you loveTaste is not mystical. It's preference, and preference has reasons — even if they're hard to name at first.
Challenge your aesthetic defaults — once a month, seriously engage with something you'd normally skipThe point isn't to change what you like. It's to make sure your tastes are chosen, not just defaults.
Who do you admire and why — write about someone you look up to and try to name exactly what quality you're responding toUsually what you admire in others is a value you hold but haven't named yet.
Phase 4
Critical Thinking Habits
Month 2 onward
Identifying logical fallacies — straw man, false dichotomy, ad hominem, appeal to authorityNot to win arguments. To notice when you're using them in your own thinking.
Cognitive biases — confirmation bias, in-group bias, availability heuristicEveryone has them. The goal is to know which ones affect you most.
Media literacy — reading news critically, distinguishing reporting from opinion, identifying framingThe skill isn't skepticism of everything — it's knowing what questions to ask about a source.
Intellectual honesty practices — updating your views when evidence changes, saying "I don't know" moreThis is rare. Most people defend whatever they believed at age 16 for the rest of their lives.
Resources
Your Logical Fallacy Is — logicalfallacies.info (free, visual)
Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman (ambitious but worth it over time)
Farnam Street Blog (fs.blog) — mental models, decision making, free essays
